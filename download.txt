In the context of systems engineering, software development, and AI, INCOSE Quality Rules refer to the set of standards defined in the INCOSE Guide for Writing Requirements.
While EARS (which we discussed previously) focuses on syntax (sentence structure), the INCOSE rules focus on semantics and quality. They are a checklist of roughly 40+ strict rules designed to ensure a requirement is actually verifiable and unambiguous.
1. The Core Objective
The International Council on Systems Engineering (INCOSE) created these rules to stop "fuzzy" engineering. In traditional software, a vague requirement leads to bugs. In AI and autonomous systems, a vague requirement leads to safety failures.
The "Golden Rule" of INCOSE is: If you can't test it, you can't write it.
2. Key INCOSE Rules (The "Don'ts")
The guide includes many specific rules, but they generally fall into these critical categories often used to train AI compliance checkers:
Category
	The Rule
	Why it matters for AI/Software
	Ambiguity
	Avoid "Weak" Words
	Words like fast, user-friendly, robust, sufficient, approximate are banned. You must use specific numbers (e.g., "under 50ms").
	Singularity
	One Thought Per Requirement
	Do not use "and" or "or" to combine multiple requirements. If a test fails, you won't know which part failed.
	Mandatory
	Use "Shall" Correctly
	Shall = binding requirement. Should = goal/preference. Will = statement of fact. Confusion here is fatal in contracts.
	Verification
	Avoid Unverifiable Terms
	You cannot verify "The AI shall understand the user." You can verify "The AI shall classify the user intent with >95% accuracy."
	Passive Voice
	Use Active Voice
	Bad: "The data shall be processed." (By whom? The cloud? The chip?)


Good: "The Inference Engine shall process the data."
	3. Relevance to AI and Software
The intersection of INCOSE rules and AI is currently a very hot topic in systems engineering (Systems Engineering + AI).
A. Specifying the "Unspecifiable" (AI Safety)
AI models are often "black boxes." You cannot easily write a line of code that says if (unsafe) stop(). Therefore, the requirements for the system containing the AI must be incredibly rigorous.
* Traditional: "The car should drive safely." (Violates INCOSE rules: "safely" is vague).
* INCOSE Compliant: "The Autonomous Driving System shall maintain a minimum separation of 2.0 seconds from the leading vehicle." (Specific, testable).
B. AI as the "Quality Police"
Just as with EARS, companies are now using Large Language Models (LLMs) to automate INCOSE compliance.
* The Workflow: An engineer writes a requirement. An AI tool scans it against the 40+ INCOSE rules.
* The AI Response: "Warning: You used the word 'instantaneous.' This violates INCOSE Rule C4 (Unbounded Adjectives). Please specify a time in milliseconds."
4. INCOSE vs. EARS: The Difference
It is helpful to think of them as working together:
* EARS provides the Skeleton (The sentence template).
* INCOSE provides the Muscle (The strict vocabulary and logic rules).
Example:
* Bad Requirement: "The system should be easy to use when it starts."
* EARS applied: "When the System starts, the System shall be easy to use." (Better structure, but still vague content).
* EARS + INCOSE applied: "When the System starts, the System shall load the dashboard in less than 2 seconds." (Correct structure + Measurable metric).
